There's a version of building that is about shipping. Get it out. Make it real. Move fast.

And then there's another version — quieter, slower — that is about understanding. Really understanding. The kind where you can hold the whole thing in your head at once, where you know not just what it does but why it does it that way, and what it would have to be for it to be different.

Most software exists somewhere between the two, and that's fine. But I think the best work comes from people who've spent time in both modes.

<Callout type="insight">
  The goal isn't to ship fast *or* to understand deeply. It's to develop the judgment to know which one the situation calls for.
</Callout>

## The gap between knowing and building

I've noticed that there's a particular kind of frustration that comes from knowing exactly what you want something to be, but not yet having the skills or the context to make it so. The gap between taste and ability.

The interesting thing is that this gap never fully closes. As you get better, your taste gets sharper too. The gap shifts — it narrows, it moves — but the feeling of reaching for something just out of grasp doesn't go away.

I used to find this demoralizing. Now I find it motivating. The gap is the work.

## On constraints

The most interesting constraints are usually not the technical ones. They're the human ones.

What are people actually trying to do? What do they know, and what have they had to unlearn? What are they afraid of? What would they ask for if they knew it was possible?

You can't answer these questions from inside your editor. You have to go out and look.

## Finishing

The hardest part of building something is knowing when it's done. Not because the criteria are unclear — usually they aren't — but because finishing means letting go of the ideal version that exists in your head.

The thing in your head is perfect. The thing in the world is not. And the act of building is mostly the act of making peace with that distance.

Which is maybe why the builders I admire most are the ones who can ship something imperfect and still love it. They're not deluded about its flaws. They just understand that a flawed thing that exists is worth more than a perfect thing that doesn't.

---

Something I keep coming back to: the best builders I know all share a certain relationship with patience. Not the passive kind — the active kind. The kind that keeps working even when the thing isn't working yet.

That's it. That's the whole secret.
