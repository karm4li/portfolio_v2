People misunderstand minimalism. They think it means sparse. White walls, empty shelves, a single orchid on a glass table. That is one version of it. But it's the surface version — aesthetic minimalism, which is really just a style preference.

The deeper version is about decisions. Specifically, about the discipline required to not add something.

## Addition is the default

Every system has gravity toward complexity. Software grows. Meetings multiply. Features accumulate. Processes layer on processes. This isn't entropy — it's human nature. We're problem solvers, and the easiest way to solve a problem is to add something.

The harder move is to remove something, or better yet, to not add it in the first place.

<Callout type="note">
  The best product decision I've ever seen made was someone saying "we don't need to build that" in a meeting where everyone else thought we did.
</Callout>

## What minimalism actually requires

To practice minimalism — real minimalism — you need two things that are in tension with each other.

The first is conviction. You have to believe that the simpler version is better, even when you can't fully prove it. This requires a kind of faith in the principle.

The second is humility. You have to be genuinely open to the possibility that the simple version is wrong, and that the complexity you resisted was actually necessary.

Most people have one or the other. Very few have both.

## On interfaces

The reason good interfaces are hard to design is that simplicity is not the same as obvious. An interface can be completely simple and completely baffling. 

What you're actually going for is appropriate friction — the right amount of resistance in the right places. Enough to indicate that something matters. Not so much that it feels like fighting the thing.

This is why "fewer clicks" is a bad metric for interface quality. Sometimes more clicks is better. It depends entirely on what you're asking someone to do and what they're trying to accomplish.

## The minimalism of writing

My favorite form of minimalism is in writing. The sentence with nothing extra in it. Every word load-bearing. The one where you couldn't remove a word without losing something.

Most of my drafts are not like this. But the goal is there.

---

Anyway. The point is: minimalism is a discipline, not an aesthetic. You can have a lot of things and practice it. You can have bare walls and not practice it at all.

It's about the decision, every time, to ask: does this need to be here?
